Chapter 1

Unwrapping the Gift — IT & Ethics

Based on A Gift of Fire (5th ed.) by Sara Baase & Timothy M. Henry. This chapter introduces the rapid pace of technological change, its unexpected consequences, the recurring themes of technology challenges, and foundational ethical frameworks.

The Pace of Change
  • 1940s — First computer was built.
  • 1956 — First hard-drive disk weighed a ton and stored only five megabytes.
  • 1991 — The Space Shuttle used a one-megahertz computer; ten years later, some cars had 100-megahertz computers.
  • Today — Speeds of several gigahertz are now standard in consumer devices.
  • Cell phones: Relatively few existed in the 1990s → ~5 billion worldwide by 2011 → 16.8 billion in 2023 → 9.5 million in Jordan alone as of early 2025.
Change and Unexpected Developments
📱
Cell Phones — Beyond Calling

Originally used for calls. Now used for photos, music, video, email, health monitoring (diabetes), video conferencing, and AI agents. Raises issues of location tracking, camera-based privacy invasion, and distracted driving. Hands-free devices do not reduce distraction for young drivers.

🌐
Social Networking

First site: classmates.com (1995). MySpace: 100M profiles by 2006. Facebook: started at Harvard as a student directory. Social networks enable civic organizing, volunteering, and revolution — but also stalking, bullying, and jurors tweeting during trials.

✉️
Communication & the Web

1980s email was short text-only. Today billions of emails sent daily; texting/social media now preferred. Issues include spam, message explosion (CR), blurring of email etiquette vs. chat norms, and AI-generated content. Blogs evolved from amateur outlets into major news sources.

🤝
Collaboration

Wikipedia — volunteer-written encyclopedia with reliability concerns (a 14-year-old posed as a PhD contributor). Open-source programmer communities. Web watchdog groups. Risk: emotionally-driven mobs acting without due process.

🛒
E-Commerce

Amazon started in 1994 selling books. eBay enables peer auctions. Lower overhead reduced prices. PayPal emerged to solve payment trust issues. Small businesses and artists can bypass middlemen. The peer-to-peer economy was born. "Free" services often monetize user data.

🤖
Artificial Intelligence

AI performs tasks requiring human intelligence — pattern recognition, speech recognition, handwriting reading, fingerprint matching, face matching. Self-driving cars must make real-time ethical decisions (e.g., unavoidable collisions). Narrow specialized skills are easier for machines than basic human cognition.

🦾
Robots

Perform hazardous physical tasks: inspecting undersea cables, searching collapsed buildings, handling nuclear waste. Raises concerns: military use (exploding robots), job displacement, and factory automation. Ethical debate around lethal autonomous weapons.

📡
Smart Sensors & Wearables

Accelerometers / MEMS (microelectromechanical systems) detect motion, gravity, temperature, moisture. Complex software interprets sensor data. Wearware = clothing with embedded sensors. Applications: airbags, robot locomotion, laptop drop protection, health monitoring.

♿
Assistive Technology

OCR + speech synthesizers let blind people read menus/receipts. Digital prosthetics enable amputees to walk, climb stairs, and fly. Voice control lets those with motor disabilities operate computers and appliances. Brain-signal chips (Neuralink) being developed — raises questions about human individuality.

Discussion: "A Masai warrior with a smartphone and Google has access to more information than the President did 15 years ago." (Bill Gates — ~90% stat). In some regions, people have easier access to phones than toilets. What does this say about equity and the digital divide?
Discussion — Self-Driving Cars: The software must decide whom to hit when a crash is unavoidable (e.g., child in road vs. swerving into oncoming traffic). What criteria should govern such decisions? If autonomous vehicles cut fatalities by 90%, does that outweigh edge-case ethical failures?
Discussion — AI & Humanity: How will society react to fully robotic surgery? To not knowing if you're talking to a human or a machine (the Turing Test)? To brain implants that enhance memory with a search engine? Will we still be human?
Discussion — "Free" Services: Many free tools (VPNs, apps, social platforms) fund themselves by collecting and selling user data. Hidden cost = your privacy. Is anything truly free?
Discussion — Avatars & Identity: Sites like Second Life let users take on personas entirely different from their real-life selves — enabling people with disabilities to interact without disclosure. What new ethical problems and benefits does this raise?
Themes of Technology Challenges
🔄
Old Problems in a New Context

Cyberspace mirrors offline society: crime, pornography, violent games, copyright infringement, advertising, gambling, and faulty products all exist online. Technology doesn't create new moral problems — it amplifies existing ones at scale.

🧠
Adapting to New Technology

Technological change requires adaptive changes in laws, social institutions, business policies, personal skills, attitudes, and behavior. We must think in new ways — not just apply old frameworks to new tools.

⚖️
Varied Sources of Solutions

Solutions come from technology, law, market forces, social norms, and individual responsibility. No single source is sufficient. Trade-offs are unavoidable — increasing security typically reduces convenience.

🌍
Global Reach of the Internet

The net enables instant communication with anyone, anywhere. This creates jurisdictional challenges (which country's laws apply?), cultural conflicts, and new opportunities for both cooperation and harm at global scale.

Key Principle: There is a fundamental difference between personal choices, business policies, and law. Something may be legal but unethical, or ethical but not yet codified in law. These distinctions matter enormously in technology contexts.
Ethics — What Is It?

Ethics is the study of what it means to "do the right thing." It assumes people are rational and make free choices. It provides rules to guide our interactions and actions that affect others.

TheoryCore IdeaKey Concept
Deontological Emphasizes duty and absolute rules to be followed regardless of consequences. An act is ethical if it complies with ethical rules, chosen for that reason. Example: "Do not lie" — even if lying would produce a better outcome.
Utilitarianism (Consequentialism) An action's morality is judged by its outcomes. The goal is to maximize happiness/utility for the greatest number (John Stuart Mill). Act utilitarianism: judge each action individually. Rule utilitarianism: adopt general rules that maximize utility → leads to law. Raises concern: majority benefit vs. minority harm ("collateral damage").
Natural Rights Rights that come from nature or human nature — not granted by government. People have the right to self-determination without interference. Negative rights (liberties): right to act without interference (e.g., freedom of speech). Positive rights (claim-rights): an obligation on others to provide something (e.g., right to healthcare).
Golden Rule Treat others as you would want to be treated. Universal across cultures and religions. A simple but powerful guide for interpersonal ethics.
Social Contract People voluntarily submit to common law to live in civil society (Rawls). Justice is what rational people behind a "veil of ignorance" would agree to. Foundation for democratic law. Legitimacy comes from consent of the governed.
Contributing to Society Doing one's work honestly, responsibly, ethically, creatively, and well is itself a moral act. Applies directly to software developers, engineers, and IT professionals.
Ethics vs. Morals vs. Laws — Key Distinctions
Ethics
An innate, universal understanding of right/wrong that transcends culture, religion, and time. Broader than law. Studies and questions morality. Fills the gap between legal standards and specific choices. Ethics precedes law — ethical principles shape whether laws should be enacted.
Morals
Living according to a moral code — principles of right and wrong. Basic morality condemns murder, adultery, lying, and stealing. Often used interchangeably with ethics, though technically distinct. Morals are often culturally situated; ethics aims to transcend culture.
Law
Rules established by authority (government) and enforced with sanctions. Law sets minimum standards applicable to all. A good law leaves room for voluntary ethical choices above the minimum. "Letter of the law" vs. "Spirit of the law" — following the technical wording while violating the intent is legal but unethical. Not everything unethical is illegal, and not everything illegal is necessarily unethical.
  • Acts are either ethically obligatory, ethically acceptable, or ethically prohibited.
  • Harm alone is not sufficient to determine an act is unethical — context matters (e.g., "collateral damage" in war, side effects of medicine).
  • Wrong vs. harm: An act can be wrong (violates a rule) without causing harm, and can cause harm without being wrong.
  • Personal preference ≠ ethics: Disliking something personally does not make it unethical.
  • Do organizations have ethics? Ultimately, individuals make decisions — but both individuals and organizations can be held responsible. A company's ethical character depends on whether its goal-achieving actions comply with ethical constraints and laws.
  • No simple answers: Human situations are complex. Ethical theories identify important principles but rarely give clear-cut answers. Multiple theories can support opposite sides of the same issue.
  • "Erring on the side of caution" — when unsure, default to the more ethical choice. استفت قلبك (Consult your conscience).
Discussion — Riggs vs. Palmer: A grandson murdered his grandfather to inherit his estate. Legally, he should inherit — the will was valid. Ethically, courts ruled he could not benefit from his own crime. Ethics filled the gap where law alone was insufficient.
Discussion — Negative vs. Positive Rights: Freedom of speech (negative right: act without interference) conflicts with protecting children from inappropriate content (positive right: obligation to protect). Is the right to remain silent a negative or positive right?
Chapter 2

Privacy

Key Aspects of Privacy
🚪
Freedom from Intrusion

The right to be left alone — personal space and life should not be invaded without consent.

🗂️
Control of Information

Individuals should be able to determine who collects, stores, and uses data about them.

👁️
Freedom from Surveillance

Protection from being tracked, followed, or watched without knowledge or permission.

Categories of Privacy Threats
  • Intentional institutional use — Organizations deliberately exploiting personal data for commercial or governmental purposes.
  • Unauthorized insider release — Employees leaking or misusing data they have privileged access to.
  • Theft of information — Malicious actors stealing personal data via hacking or other means.
  • Inadvertent leakage — Unintentional exposure of data due to software bugs, misconfigurations, or human error.
  • Our own actions — Intentional trade-offs (giving up privacy for a benefit) and unknowing exposure due to lack of awareness.
New Technology, New Risks

Technology has dramatically expanded the scale and nature of privacy threats. Key risk factors include:

🔍 Search Query Data

Search engines collect terabytes of data daily, used for targeted advertising and service development. This data can be subpoenaed by courts or examined during visa/job applications.

📱 Smartphones

Location apps and mobile software often store and transmit data without the user's knowledge. Tests found half of apps sent device IDs or location to third parties. Some banking apps inadvertently stored account credentials in hidden files.

  • Everything done in cyberspace is recorded and stored at scale.
  • People are largely unaware of how much data is collected about them.
  • Software complexity makes securing data extremely difficult.
  • Small collected data points can be aggregated to form detailed personal profiles.
  • Re-identification has become easy due to the volume of data and powerful analysis tools.
  • Once information is public on the web, it is essentially available to everyone, permanently.
  • Data collected for one purpose routinely finds other uses.
  • Governments can request personal data held by private companies and organizations.
  • Individuals depend on the goodwill of businesses to protect their data — there is little direct control.

Informed Consent Mechanisms
Opt-Out
The organization may use data by default. The individual must take explicit action (e.g., checking a box) to prevent use. Result: more people end up "in" the data pool. Often online checkboxes are pre-checked, requiring users to actively uncheck.
Opt-In
Data may only be used if the individual explicitly grants permission (e.g., checking a box). Result: more people end up "out" of the data pool. Considered more protective of privacy.

Most privacy policies include a "subject to change without notice" clause, which undermines the meaningful nature of initial consent.

Fair Information Principles
  1. Transparency — Inform people when you collect information about them.
  2. Data Minimization — Collect only the data that is actually needed.
  3. Choice — Offer a way for people to opt out.
  4. Retention Limits — Keep data only as long as necessary.
  5. Accuracy — Maintain correctness and currency of stored data.
  6. Security — Protect data from unauthorized access or disclosure.
  7. Accountability — Develop clear policies for responding to law enforcement requests.
New Technologies & Their Privacy Implications
📷
Video Surveillance & Face Recognition

Security cameras trade privacy for security. England deployed millions of public cameras. The 2001 Super Bowl covertly scanned 100,000 faces. Studies found abuses by surveillance operators. Some cities abandoned systems as they didn't reduce crime meaningfully.

🌐
Social Networks

Users expose opinions, location ("away from home"), gossip, and photos. Platforms introduce features with unexpected privacy defaults (e.g., Facebook announcing friends' purchases). Embarrassing material is extremely difficult to permanently delete.

☁️
Cloud Computing

Cloud services offer convenience but raise serious questions about the security of personal data stored in systems outside user control, accessible to the provider and potentially to governments.

📍
Location Tracking (GPS)

GPS and cell-based services know exactly where a person is at any moment. Both benefits (navigation, emergency services) and serious risks (surveillance, stalking) exist.

Government Systems
Key Issues: Governments use data mining and computer matching to fight terrorism and crime. Public records (bankruptcy, property, arrest records, government salaries) are accessible by definition but create identity theft risks. National ID systems like the US Social Security Number (introduced 1936) are widely used, easily falsified, and viewed by critics as threats to freedom — reminiscent of demands for "papers" in authoritarian regimes.

Two fundamental problems arise from new technologies in the government context: (1) personal information increasingly resides in large external databases outside individual control; (2) governments can now search homes and persons remotely and covertly using technology.

Chapter 3

Legal, Ethical & Professional Issues in Information Security

Why This Matters

Information security professionals must understand their organization's legal and ethical responsibilities. To minimize risks, they must: (1) understand the current legal environment — laws, policies, bylaws, and regulations; (2) stay current with evolving laws; (3) watch for emerging issues. Failure can result in significant financial and legal liabilities for individuals and organizations.

Law vs. Ethics
⚖️ Law

Written rules that mandate or prohibit behavior, enforced by the state/a third party. Violations result in financial penalties or custodial sentences. Law is narrower in scope than ethics.

🧭 Ethics

Defines socially acceptable behavior, driven by personal values, morals, and cultural norms. Generally unwritten. Violations are not formally punished — only enforced by conscience. Broader than law.

ConceptDefinition
Cultural MoresFixed moral attitudes or customs of a particular group; the shared behavioral standards of a society.
EthicsSocially acceptable behavior; what a society collectively considers right or wrong.
LawsRules that mandate or prohibit certain behavior, enforced by the state, carrying formal sanctions.
Organizational Liability

Organizations must ensure every employee understands acceptable and unacceptable behavior to meet legal obligations. Reasons include:

  • To maintain the reputation of the company and its employees.
  • To preserve the legal rights of the company, employees, and customers.
  • Because if an employee performs an illegal or unethical act causing harm, the employer can be held financially liable — organizations spend enormous effort minimizing this risk.
Policy in Information Security

Policy is the list of expectations describing acceptable and unacceptable employee behavior. For a policy to be legally enforceable, it must meet all five criteria:

  1. Dissemination — The policy must be made easily available to employees (hard copy, electronic distribution).
  2. Review — The policy must be distributed in an understandable form; multiple languages if needed.
  3. Comprehension — The organization must demonstrate employees understand the content (quizzes, assessments).
  4. Compliance — Employees must affirmatively agree to the policy via logon banners (requiring a click) or signed documents.
  5. Uniform Enforcement — The policy must be enforced consistently, regardless of employment status or role.
Important: Only when all five conditions are satisfied can an organization penalize policy violators without fear of legal retribution.
Types of Law
🏛️
Civil Law

Governs relationships between individuals and organizations. Includes contract law, employment law, family law, and tort law. Tort law enables individuals to seek compensation for personal, physical, or financial injury.

🚔
Criminal Law

Addresses violations deemed harmful to society — traffic law, public order, property damage, personal harm. Cases are brought by the government (prosecution) against a defendant.

👨‍👩‍👧
Private Law

A subset of civil law regulating relationships among individuals and between individuals and organizations. Includes family law, commercial law, and labor law.

🏛️
Public Law

Regulates government structure and its relationships with citizens, employees, and other governments. Includes criminal law, administrative law, and constitutional law.

Privacy in the Context of Information Security

Privacy in information security is the right of individuals or groups to protect themselves and their information from unauthorized access, providing confidentiality. Common privacy violations include:

  • Organizations collecting, swapping, and selling personal data (name, address, phone, location) as a commodity — driving calls for legal protections.
  • Eavesdropping on private communications — phone calls, messages, emails.
Chapter 4

Intellectual Property Rights & Patents

What Are Intellectual Property Rights?

Intellectual Property Rights (IPRs) are legal mechanisms that protect the works and inventions of creators worldwide. They grant the right to use, license, and profit from one's creations. IPRs are critical for motivating innovation — global patent registrations grew 3.6% from 2020 to 2021.

™️
Trademarks

Protect words, symbols, and designs that distinguish a company's goods or services. Can last indefinitely if actively used. Example: Nike's "Just Do It."

©️
Copyrights

Protect original creative works — books, music, movies, software. Gives creators control over who can copy, share, or perform their work. Can last beyond the creator's death.

🔒
Trade Secrets

Guard sensitive data that gives a business a competitive advantage. Protected as long as they remain valuable and undisclosed. Examples: KFC recipe, Coca-Cola formula.

📋
Patents

Protect new, useful, and non-obvious inventions. Grant the inventor exclusive rights (~20 years) in exchange for public disclosure of the invention. Examples: Edison's lightbulb, Bell's telephone.

Patents — Deep Dive

A patent grants its owner the exclusive legal authority to make, use, sell, or import their invention for a fixed period. In return, the inventor must publicly disclose how the invention works. This distinguishes patents from trade secrets.

Three Core Patent Requirements:
Novelty — The invention must be entirely new and previously undisclosed (in Jordan: absolute novelty, meaning not used or published anywhere in the world).
Utility — The invention must have a real-world, practical use and be capable of industrial application.
Non-Obviousness — The invention must not be trivial or obvious to a person of ordinary skill in the relevant field.
Types of Patents
🔧
Utility Patents

Most common — over two-thirds of all patents. Cover functional inventions. Three subtypes: General utility (functionality), Specific utility (how it carries out the function), Moral utility (invention must not enable harm). Annual maintenance fees apply with a 6-month grace period.

🎨
Design Patents

Protect the ornamental/aesthetic appearance of an object — chairs, footwear, fonts, computer icons, curved UI edges. The design must be aesthetically pleasing and inseparable from the object. Lifespan: 15 years. No maintenance fees required.

🌱
Plant Patents

Cover invented or discovered asexually reproduced plant varieties (grafting, cutting — not seed-based). Examples: Strawberry Plant "Aroma," Grape "La Crescent." Lifespan: 20 years. No maintenance costs.

Process of Obtaining a Patent
  1. Submit a patent application to the relevant government body, fully describing the invention, its function, and how it differs from existing solutions.
  2. Include detailed claims — precise declarations of what is considered patentable.
  3. A technical examiner is assigned based on the nature of the invention.
  4. Novelty is verified — the patent office may contact foreign offices to confirm no duplicate exists.
  5. Patent is granted (or rejected) based on the novelty, utility, and non-obviousness criteria.
Important: There is no worldwide patent. Patents are territorial — valid only in the country where registered. To protect an invention internationally, it must be filed individually in each country within 12 months of the original registration. A Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) extends this window to 30 months but costs $3,700–$4,500.
Patent Registration in Jordan
  • Applications must be submitted in person at the Ministry of Industry, Trade, and Supplies — no online application available.
  • A proxy or licensed industrial property agent may act on the applicant's behalf (with notarized power of attorney).
  • The Jordanian patent office verifies novelty by contacting foreign patent offices.
  • Total registration cost: 150 JD (50 JD registration + 50 JD Official Gazette publication + 50 JD final certificate).
  • Required documents include: ID card, detailed invention description, drawings, sworn statement, power of attorney, priority right certificate if applicable.
  • Patent protection duration: 20 years from the application date.
  • Annual maintenance fees are required, with a 6-month grace period for late payment.
IP Across Different Fields
🔐
Cybersecurity

IP is a critical asset for companies like PepsiCo and Microsoft. The CIA triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) protects IP: AES-128/RSA for confidentiality; DSA/Public Key for integrity; SCTP to mitigate DDoS for availability.

🎮
Video Games

Multiple IP types apply: Copyright for characters, art, music, story; Trademarks for brand identity; Patents for gameplay innovations; Trade Secrets for design strategies; Licensing for borrowed IP; Digital Rights for online distribution.

🌍
Websites

Websites rely on Copyright for content, Trademarks for names/logos, unique Domain Names, Privacy Policies for data handling, Open Source licensing rules, and Patents for underlying technology methods.

IP Advantages & Disadvantages

✅ Advantages

  • Competitive Edge — Competitors cannot simply copy protected innovations.
  • Enhanced Company Value — IP assets significantly increase business worth for investors and buyers.
  • Income Generation — IP can be licensed for royalties or sold outright.
  • Marketing & Branding — Trademarks differentiate businesses, build trust, and attract customers.
  • Financing — IP assets can serve as collateral for loans or attract investment.

❌ Disadvantages

  • High Costs — Legal fees, filing fees, and maintenance fees can be prohibitively expensive, especially for complex inventions.
  • Difficult to Enforce — Stopping copying or theft is challenging; enforcement actions can alienate customers and harm reputation.
  • Reduced Competition — Patent monopolies can result in oligopolies, fewer consumer choices, and higher prices.
  • Barriers to Innovation — Patents can block incremental improvements or new entrants from building on existing ideas.
Ethical & Legal Considerations

While patents aim to incentivize innovation, they raise significant ethical and legal questions affecting consumers, competitors, researchers, and society:

💊
Pharmaceuticals

Patents on life-saving medications create high prices inaccessible to low-income and developing-nation populations. This restricts generic alternatives. Example: insulin pricing controversies in the US. (Frederick Banting sold insulin's patent for $1 in 1923.)

💻
Software Industry

Patents protect against unauthorized copying but can restrict how existing code is used and modified, limiting developer creativity and collaboration. Patent litigation is costly and time-consuming.

🧬
Biotechnology

Patents on biological processes and natural resources raise issues about ownership of native knowledge, biodiversity, and environmental sustainability. Example: Monsanto's controversies over genetically modified seed patents.

🤖
AI & Digital Media

Emerging battles: Getty Images vs. Stability AI (Stable Diffusion trained on copyrighted images without consent); RED Digital Cinema vs. competitors over compressed RAW video patents. Artists and companies seek billions in damages.

Conclusion: IPRs are essential for economic growth, innovation, and protecting creators' investments. However, the ethical implications — particularly around access, monopoly power, and knowledge sharing — require ongoing attention from lawmakers, businesses, and society. Jordan and other countries continue to develop more efficient, digitally accessible patent systems to support inventors.
